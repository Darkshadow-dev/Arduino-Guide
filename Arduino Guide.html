LEDs (Light Emitting Diodes)

LEDs are semiconductor devices that emit light when an electric current passes through them. Invented in the early 1960s, LEDs have revolutionized lighting and display technologies due to their efficiency, long lifespan, and small size.

History

The first visible-spectrum LED was created in 1962 by Nick Holonyak Jr., often called the "father of the LED." Initially, LEDs emitted only red light and were primarily used as indicator lights. Over the decades, advances produced LEDs in many colors, including white, enabling their use in lighting, displays, and more.

How LEDs Work

LEDs allow current to flow in one direction â€” from the positive anode (longer leg) to the negative cathode (shorter leg). When electrons recombine with holes inside the semiconductor material, energy is released in the form of photons (light).

Image from BYJUs

Usage and Applications
  • Indicator lights in electronics
  • Displays and screens
  • Lighting (homes, streetlights, automotive)
  • Optical communication
  • Decorative and wearable tech
Important Tips
  • Polarity matters: The longer leg is the anode (+), and the shorter leg is the cathode (âˆ’). Connecting LEDs backward prevents them from lighting.
  • Use a current-limiting resistor: LEDs require a resistor in series to prevent excessive current, which can damage the LED or your Arduino.
  • Common Ground: You can connect all negative legs of LEDs, displays, and motors to a common GND pin. However, avoid overloading a single GND line with too many devices.
  • Calculate resistor values: Use Ohmâ€™s law to pick the right resistor based on your supply voltage and LED forward voltage/current.
Common Mistakes
  • Connecting LEDs without resistors â€” leads to burning out
  • Reversing polarity â€” LED wonâ€™t light
  • Connecting too many LEDs to one GND line causing unstable circuits

Resistors

Resistors are passive electrical components that limit or regulate the flow of electrical current in a circuit. They are essential for protecting sensitive components and controlling voltage and current levels.

History

The resistor concept dates back to the 19th century, following the discovery of Ohmâ€™s Law by Georg Simon Ohm in 1827. Since then, resistors have become fundamental components in virtually all electronic devices.

How Resistors Work

Resistors oppose the flow of electric current, dissipating energy as heat. Their resistance is measured in ohms (Î©), and the amount of resistance determines how much current is reduced.

Image from Utmel

Usage and Applications
  • Limit current to LEDs and other components
  • Create voltage dividers
  • Set bias points in transistors and amplifiers
  • Pull-up or pull-down resistors in digital circuits
  • Signal conditioning and filtering
Important Tips
  • Know your resistance: Check the resistor's value using color codes or a multimeter before use.
  • Power rating matters: Ensure the resistor can handle the power (watts) it will dissipate.
  • Use appropriate tolerance: Higher precision resistors are important in sensitive circuits.
  • Don't overload resistors: Excess heat can damage them or cause circuit failure.
Common Mistakes
  • Using wrong resistance value causing incorrect current flow
  • Ignoring power rating leading to resistor overheating
  • Not considering tolerance causing circuit inaccuracies
  • Connecting resistors in the wrong configuration (series vs parallel)
Remember :

Resistors limit current and protect components by controlling voltage and current flow.

Always verify resistor values and power ratings before integrating into your circuit.

Capacitors

Capacitors store and release electrical energy in a circuit. They consist of two conductive plates separated by an insulator, and are widely used for filtering, timing, and energy storage.

History

The capacitor's origins date to the 18th century with the invention of the Leyden jar. Modern capacitors became common in the 20th century, enabling advances in radio, computers, and power electronics.

How Capacitors Work

Capacitors accumulate electric charge on their plates when voltage is applied. They resist changes in voltage and can smooth power supplies, store energy briefly, or block direct current while allowing alternating current.

Image from www.aictech-inc.com

Usage and Applications
  • Power supply filtering and smoothing
  • Timing circuits with resistors
  • Coupling and decoupling signals
  • Energy storage in flash cameras and power electronics
  • Tuning circuits in radios
Important Tips
  • Observe polarity: Electrolytic capacitors have polarity; reversing them can damage the capacitor.
  • Check voltage rating: Never exceed the capacitorâ€™s rated voltage.
  • Choose correct type: Different capacitor types suit different uses (ceramic, electrolytic, film, etc.).
  • Discharge before handling: Capacitors can hold charge even after power is offâ€”discharge safely.
Common Mistakes
  • Reversing polarity on electrolytic capacitors
  • Using capacitors with voltage ratings too low for the circuit
  • Not discharging capacitors before working on a circuit
  • Incorrect type choice causing poor performance or failure
Remember :

Capacitors store electrical energy and are essential for filtering, timing, and energy management in circuits.

Always check polarity and voltage ratings when using capacitors.

Wires

Wires are conductors used to connect components in an electronic circuit. The choice of wire affects current capacity, signal integrity, and flexibility.

History

Copper wire has been used since the early days of electricity. Over time, wire insulation and different gauges have improved safety and efficiency in electronic designs.

How Wires Work

Wires carry electric current between components. The thickness (gauge) determines how much current can safely flow without excessive heating or voltage drop.

Image from InterNACHI

Usage and Applications
  • Connecting sensors, modules, and power supply
  • Prototyping with breadboards and jumper wires
  • Signal transmission in data and audio cables
  • Power wiring in devices and appliances
  • Grounding and shielding to reduce noise
Important Tips
  • Choose the right gauge: Thicker wires carry more current safely.
  • Use proper insulation: Prevent shorts and protect from environmental damage.
  • Keep wires organized: Use color coding and tidy routing for easier debugging.
  • Avoid long runs without boosting voltage: Long wires can cause voltage drops.
Common Mistakes
  • Using wires too thin for the current load causing overheating
  • Poor insulation leading to shorts or damage
  • Messy wiring causing confusion and errors
  • Not securing wires, leading to loose connections
Remember :

Wires connect your circuit components and must be chosen carefully to ensure safety and performance.

Use proper gauge and insulation for your projectâ€™s requirements.

Breadboards

3D Breadboard

A breadboard is a reusable prototyping board that allows you to build and test electronic circuits without soldering. It is one of the most important tools for learning electronics and Arduino.

History

The term â€œbreadboardâ€ comes from early electronics hobbyists who literally built circuits on wooden breadboards in the 1970s. Modern breadboards use metal spring clips under the plastic holes to make temporary electrical connections.

How a Breadboard Works

More on How

Inside a breadboard, metal strips connect groups of holes together. When you push a wire or component leg into a hole, it electrically connects to all other holes in the same strip.

Power Rails

The long lines on the sides are called power rails. They are usually marked with red (+) and blue (âˆ’).

  • Red rail â†’ connect to 5V or 3.3V
  • Blue rail â†’ connect to GND
  • Some breadboards have broken rails in the middle
Center Rows

The middle area is split into two sides. Each row of 5 holes is connected vertically.

  • Left side rows are separate from right side
  • Used for placing ICs and components
  • The center gap holds DIP chips (like microcontrollers)
Usage and Applications
  • Testing Arduino projects
  • Learning electronics
  • Temporary circuit building
  • Debugging hardware
  • Classroom and hobby projects
Important Tips
  • Always connect GND and power rails first
  • Use short jumper wires to avoid noise
  • Double-check which holes are connected
  • Label power rails when using multiple voltages
Common Mistakes
  • Assuming all long rails are connected end to end
  • Placing both legs of a component in the same row
  • Forgetting to connect the ground rail
  • Shorting power rails together
Remember:

Breadboards do not provide power by themselves â€” they only connect wires. You must supply power from the Arduino or an external source.

How Breadboards Work (In Detail)

A breadboard works by internally connecting holes using hidden metal clips. These clips allow electrical current to flow between connected points without soldering.

Internal Connections

Under the plastic surface, thin metal strips run beneath specific rows and columns. When a wire or component leg is inserted, it is clamped by a metal contact.

Row Connections

In the center area, each row of 5 holes is electrically connected. This allows components like resistors or LEDs to share voltage or signals.

  • Connections run vertically
  • Left and right sides are isolated
  • Center gap separates IC pins
Power Distribution

Power rails distribute voltage across the board. These rails make it easy to supply power to many components.

  • Red rail = VCC (5V / 3.3V)
  • Blue rail = GND
  • Rails may be split in the middle
Why Breadboards Are Safe for Learning
  • No soldering required
  • Easy to change circuits
  • Low risk of permanent damage
  • Reusable for thousands of insertions
Limitations
  • Not suitable for high current
  • Not reliable for high-frequency signals
  • Loose wires can cause unstable behavior
Important:

Breadboards are designed for testing and learning. Final projects should be soldered to perfboards or PCBs.
